# Beagle Bros Skill

**Beagle Bros Micro Software** (San Diego, founded 1980 by Bert Kersey) made Apple II
utilities with a sensibility almost nobody else had: software should be _fun_, your
machine should be _demystified_, and your customer is a _collaborator_, not a thief to
be copy-protected into submission. Their manuals were funnier than most comic books and
more useful than most textbooks. Their packaging looked like a Victorian patent-medicine
catalog. They taught a generation to PEEK, POKE, and tinker.

This skill brings that sensibility into Eric's work. It is **not** a 1980s-nostalgia
filter you smear on everything — it's a small set of principles (warmth, generosity,
demystification, irreverence aimed _up_ at pomposity and never _down_ at the user) plus
the concrete voice/look to execute them.

Use it for: README/docs/changelog/release-note voice, CLI and error copy, splash
screens, easter eggs, naming, and retro-aesthetic layout. This file is an index — load
the one reference that fits.

## How to apply (the short version)

1. **Lead with the substance.** Beagle Bros docs were genuinely _correct and useful_ —
   the jokes rode alongside real technical content, never replaced it. A funny README
   that lies is just a lie wearing a top hat.
2. **Punch up, never down.** Tease the machine, the bureaucracy, the absurdity, and
   yourself. Never the reader for not knowing something.
3. **Be generous.** Hand over the trick. Show the PEEK. Explain _why_, not just _how_.
4. **Trust the user.** No condescension, no gatekeeping, minimal friction.
5. **Season, don't drown.** One good gag per section, then get out of the way.

If the task is purely factual ("what did Beagle Bros make?") just read `history.md` and
answer. If it's "make this _feel_ Beagle Bros," pull `voice.md` and/or `aesthetic.md`.

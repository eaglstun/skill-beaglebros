# Beagle Bros — the facts

The factual anchor. When you invoke the _sensibility_, keep it honest by keeping it
attached to what really existed. Don't invent products, slogans, or people.

## The company

- **Beagle Bros Micro Software**, San Diego, California. Founded **1980** by **Bert
  Kersey**. (Despite the name there were no actual "brothers" — it was Kersey's joke.)
- Published software for the **Apple II family** — II, II+, IIe, IIc, and later IIgs.
  Almost exclusively Apple II; that was the whole identity.
- Key collaborators over the years included **Mark Simonsen** (who took on a leading
  role as the company grew) and a stable of programmers; the cartoon/jokey house voice
  ran through everything.
- The later **TimeOut** add-on line for AppleWorks was the big commercial success of the
  company's second act. As the Apple II faded at the end of the 1980s, the business and
  the TimeOut line were sold to **Quality Computers** (early 1990s). Treat exact dates
  loosely unless you verify them — the _spirit_ is the point here, not trivia precision.

## What they actually shipped (real titles)

Utilities and tools — small, sharp, single-purpose, with personality:

- **DOS Boss** — customize Apple DOS 3.3: rename commands, change the prompt, protect
  programs. The "rename CATALOG to whatever you want" trick is peak Beagle Bros.
- **Pronto-DOS** — a faster-loading replacement DOS.
- **Apple Mechanic** — hi-res shape/graphics tool.
- **GPLE (Global Program Line Editor)** — a beloved BASIC line editor.
- **Beagle Basic** — Applesoft enhancements.
- **Utility City** — a grab-bag of 21 utility programs.
- **Alpha Plot** — hi-res graphics + text.
- **Frame-Up** — hi-res slideshow/presentation tool.
- **Silicon Salad** — collection of utilities (a follow-up grab-bag).
- **Tip Disk #1** — programming tips, on disk.
- **Flex Type**, **Triple-Dump**, **Big U**, **Disk Quik** (RAM disk), **D Code**,
  **Fatcat**, **I.O. Silver**, **Beagle Graphics**, **Beagle Compiler**.
- **TimeOut series** for AppleWorks — SuperFonts, SideSpread, QuickSpell, UltraMacros,
  Graph, DeskTools, and more. The second-act hit.

If you're not sure a title is real, say so or check — don't bluff a fake one into a doc.

## What they pioneered (the part worth stealing)

- **Documentation as entertainment.** Manuals packed with cartoons, jokes, and sidebars
  — but genuinely instructive. People _kept_ and _re-read_ Beagle Bros manuals.
- **The Peeks & Pokes chart.** A famous reference poster/card of useful Apple II memory
  locations (PEEK/POKE/CALL) — generosity as marketing: here, learn your machine.
- **Friendly attitude toward users.** Comparatively light, good-humored copy protection
  in an era of paranoid, customer-hostile schemes. "We're the good guys" energy.
- **The Victorian-engraving look.** Top hats, pointing hands, mustaches, ornate borders
  — ad copy that looked like a 19th-century mail-order catalog. Instantly recognizable.
- **Easter eggs and hidden jokes** baked into the software itself.

## One-line summary

Tiny, joyful, generous tools that respected and taught their users — wrapped in
old-timey woodcut packaging and the funniest manuals in the business.
